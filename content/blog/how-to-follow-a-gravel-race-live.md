Somewhere right now, someone is refreshing a map. Their person is a dot on it — halfway up a climb in Kansas or Girona or Northumberland — and the dot is all they have. This guide is for them, and for anyone who has heard the word *dotwatching* and wondered what there could possibly be to watch.

Quite a lot, it turns out.

## First, get the link.

Every race tracked on Aster has a watch page. If you know someone riding, ask them for their link before the start — riders can share it from the app in one tap. If you're following the race itself, the event's page lists everyone. Either way, it opens in a browser. No app, no account, no signup.

## Reading the map.

- **The dot is the rider, now.** Not at the last checkpoint — now. If it's moving, they're moving.
- **The trail behind it** is where they've been. A tidy line along the route is a good day. A loop back on itself usually means a missed turn, and yes, they know.
- **The leaderboard** shows position, distance covered, and the gap to the rider ahead. Gaps are where the racing lives.

## What the gaps mean.

A gravel race rarely changes hands in a sprint. It changes hands slowly, over an hour, and the leaderboard shows it happening before it happens. If the gap from first to second has shrunk three times in a row, you're watching a catch unfold. If it's grown all afternoon, you're watching someone win a race by themselves, which has its own bleak beauty.

This is the real skill of dotwatching: not staring, but *sampling*. Check in, read the trend, put the phone down, come back in twenty minutes to see if you were right.

## When the dot stops.

Dots stop. Riders fix punctures, queue for water, sit in ditches eating flapjack and questioning their choices. A stopped dot is almost always nothing — and if a rider is stopped for a long time, the race organiser sees the same thing you do, sooner. If you'd rather not check at all, turn on notifications for your rider and the map will tap you on the shoulder when something actually happens: they've started, they're moving again, they're two kilometres from the line.

## Be at the finish.

This is the part checkpoint timing never gave anyone: you can see the finish coming. When your rider turns for home, the map knows, you know, and — if you're the kind of person who drives to a field in the dark to hold a cowbell — you can be standing in exactly the right place when they arrive.

That's the whole guide. One link, a little patience, and a day that used to be silence becomes a day you were quietly there for all of it.
