There are two race days. There's the one out on the course — the gun, the climbs, the food stops, the finish. And there's the one at home: a kitchen table, a phone face-up next to the teapot, and hours of not knowing.

Most events have only ever fixed the first one. The second one — the parent, the partner, the housemate who drove them to the start at 5am — gets a text at registration, maybe a checkpoint time in the afternoon, and then silence until the finish photo arrives.

Live tracking fixes the second race day. Here's what actually changes.

## One link is the whole setup.

When a rider shares their live track, the person at home gets a link. That's it. It opens in any browser, on any device — no app to install, no account to create, no password to reset at the worst possible moment. Grandparents can do it. The group chat can do it. The whole point is that following someone should be easier than texting them.

## The relief of movement.

Ask anyone who has followed a long race from home and they'll tell you the same thing: the map matters less than the motion. A position that updates is a message in itself. *They're moving. They're okay.*

That's the thing checkpoint timing never gave you. A checkpoint tells you where someone was — forty minutes ago, an hour ago, whenever they last crossed a timing mat. Between mats, you're guessing. Did they puncture? Did they stop? Is the gap between CP2 and CP3 normal, or should someone be worried?

With a live track there's no gap to fill with worry. The trail draws itself across the map, the position moves, and the silence between checkpoints stops being silence.

## Checkpoints tell you what happened. Live tells you what's happening.

Checkpoint splits are still useful — they're how you compare, how you pace, how results get settled. Live tracking doesn't replace them; it fills in everything between them.

- **The chase, as it unfolds.** Thirty seconds down to the group ahead and closing is a story. A results PDF the next morning is a summary.
- **The moments that matter.** You see them start the final climb. You see them turn for home. You can be at the finish line — actually at it, or emotionally at it — at the right moment, because you watched them approach.
- **The finish, live.** The instant they cross the line, everyone following knows. Not when they get signal back, not when they remember to text. The moment it happens.

## What this costs the person at home.

Nothing. Watching is free, always — no subscription, no trial, no account. The rider tracks from the phone already in their pocket; the watcher opens a link. That's the entire transaction.

Organisers who add live tracking to their events tell us the same thing afterwards: the finish line gets busier. People turn up at the right time because they knew when to come. The event gets shared while it's happening, not after. And the riders — who spent the whole day being watched over — come back next year.

If someone you love is racing this weekend, ask them for the link.
